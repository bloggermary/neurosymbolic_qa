Diagnosing diabetes in older adults taking multiple medications requires more caution than in younger patients, because both presentation and test reliability are affected by age, comorbidity, and drug interactions.

The core diagnostic thresholds remain unchanged in principle: fasting plasma glucose of 126 mg/dL or higher, random plasma glucose of 200 mg/dL or higher, 2-hour OGTT of 200 mg/dL or higher, or HbA1c of 6.5% or higher. However, HbA1c can be unreliable in patients with chronic kidney disease or anemia, so ask whether the patient has either condition (boolean) before relying heavily on the HbA1c result.

Classic symptoms - excessive thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, blurred vision - are frequently absent or attributed to other causes in elderly patients, so their absence should not be reassuring. Still ask about each as a yes/no question, but weight a negative answer less heavily than in a younger adult.

Medication use is a major confounder. Ask which of the following the patient currently takes, allowing more than one to apply conceptually but recording the primary contributor as a category: corticosteroids, thiazide_diuretics, atypical_antipsychotics, none. Each of these can raise blood glucose independent of true diabetes.

Renal function should be captured as a plain numeric value: estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) in mL/min/1.73m2. Ask how many distinct chronic conditions the patient has (a plain count), since a higher comorbidity burden changes how aggressively glucose targets should be pursued.

Cognitive and functional status should be rated on a scale from 1 (fully independent) to 10 (fully dependent), since this materially affects what diagnostic follow-up and treatment plan is realistic.

Ask how many years the patient has had any known glucose abnormality, if applicable, as this history changes the interpretation of a new borderline result versus a first-time finding.
