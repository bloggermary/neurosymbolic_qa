A diagnosis of diabetes mellitus is established if at least one of the following criteria is met:

Random plasma glucose ≥ 11.1 mmol/L (200 mg/dL).
Fasting plasma glucose ≥ 7.0 mmol/L (126 mg/dL) after 8–12 hours of fasting.
2-hour plasma glucose during an oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) ≥ 11.1 mmol/L (200 mg/dL).
HbA1c ≥ 6.5% (48 mmol/mol).




Prediabetes

Prediabetes may be present if:
Fasting plasma glucose is between 100 and 125 mg/dL.

Common symptoms of prediabetes include:
- excessive thirst
- excessive urination
- fatigue
- blurred vision

Common symptoms associated with diabetes include excessive thirst,
frequent urination, fatigue, and blurred vision. Less commonly, patients
may also report unexplained weight loss, increased hunger, slow-healing
wounds or frequent infections, and tingling or numbness in the hands or
feet. These additional symptoms are not required for every evaluation -
they are only worth asking about when the core symptom picture and
numeric evidence are not already sufficient to reach a conclusion.

A symptom pattern is considered strongly supportive when excessive thirst
and frequent urination are both present. One of these symptoms alone
provides partial support.

Body mass index (BMI), waist circumference, resting blood pressure, and
a family history of diabetes are additional risk factors clinicians may
record as part of a full workup. None of these on their own confirms or
rules out diabetes, and they do not need to be collected every time - they
mainly help clarify risk in borderline or ambiguous cases.

Symptoms present for fewer than 7 days are considered recent.
Symptoms present for 7 to 30 days are considered persistent.
Symptoms present for more than 30 days are considered long-term.

Fatigue severity is assessed on a whole-number scale from 1 to 10:

- 1 to 3 indicates mild fatigue.
- 4 to 6 indicates moderate fatigue.
- 7 to 10 indicates severe fatigue.

Thirst severity may be described as none, mild, moderate, or severe.

Rather than asking about each symptom one at a time, the clinician may
instead present the full symptom checklist at once - excessive thirst,
excessive urination, fatigue, and blurred vision - and ask the patient
to select all of the ones that currently apply.

When more than one symptom is present, ask the patient to list the
symptoms in the order they first appeared, from earliest to most
recent.

If the patient is currently taking any diabetes medication, record
each medication as a single structured entry: the medication's name,
its dose in milligrams, and how many times per day it is taken.
